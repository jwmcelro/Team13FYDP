Formatting

Choosing the correct fonts, colours, and formatting ensures legibility and readability for all students.

  • Microsoft PowerPoint
  • Microsoft Word
  • Google Slides
  • Google Docs

Microsoft PowerPoint

This workflow includes the following tasks: best practices, choosing a font, choosing a colour, inserting hyperlink text.

1. Best Practices

- Include navigation landmarks: Adding landmarks such as page numbers and proper headings helps to ensure students using screen readers understand where they are on a page.

- Don't use colour alone to convey information: Students who are blind and/or have low vision and using a screen reader may not be able to decipher between colours.

- Avoid excessive use of bold and italicized text: Bold and italics should only be used to highlight important content as students with dyslexia may struggle with italics and screen readers do not interpret bold and italicized text.

- Include a descriptive title for each slide: This gives students using screen readers proper awareness of the content that is being read to them.

2. Choosing the correct Fonts

Ensures that slides have no distractive or hard to read text for students with ADHD, low vision, dyslexia and more.

Step 1: Highlight your text and select a Sans-Serif Font, for example, Arial, Verdana or Calibri. Sans Serif fonts have clean lines and sharp edges to increase readability.

Step 2: It is recommended to use fonts larger than 28-points. Select text and choose a font size of 28 or larger.

Step 3: Outline text on slides with a text box as text boxes are not read aloud by all screen readers. To do so select the text box click "Shape Format" then select "Shape Outline" and choose a colour.

3. Choosing a Colour

Using high contrast colours ensures that text is legible and not the only means of conveying information, which helps to accommodate students with visual disabilities like colour blindness. Ex) Dark font on light background, light font on dark background, opposing colours on colour wheel, etc.

Step 1: Select your text and choose a high contrast colour in relation to the background from the standard colour options in the Home Menu.


4. Inserting Hyperlink Text

Ensures that students students who are blind and/or have low vision can read and understand what the link is meant to be used for.

Step 1: Prepare a short description of the link in a few words.



Step 2: Highlight the portion of text you want to link, right click, and select "Link".



Step 3: Add in the link to the address box and select "OK".

Microsoft Word

This workflow includes the following tasks: best practices, choosing a font, choosing a colour, inserting hyperlink text, heading structure.

1. Best Practices

- Include navigation landmarks: Adding landmarks such as page numbers and proper headings helps to ensure students using screen readers understand where they are on a page.

- Don't use colour alone to convey information: Students who are blind and/or have low vision and using a screen reader may not be able to decipher between colours.

- Avoid excessive use of bold and italicized text: Bold and italics should only be used to highlight important content as students with dyslexia may struggle with italics and screen readers do not interpret bold and italicized text.

- Include a descriptive title for each document section: This gives students using screen readers proper awareness of the content that is being read to them.

2. Choosing a Font

Ensures that text has no distractive or hard to read text for students with ADHD, low vision, dyslexia and more.

Step 1: Highlight your text and select a Sans-Serif Font, for example, Arial, Verdana or Calibri. Sans Serif fonts have clean lines and sharp edges to increase readability.

Step 2: It is recommended to use fonts larger than 11-points. Select text and choose a font size of 11 or larger.

3. Choosing a Colour

Using high contrast colours ensures that text is legible and not the only means of conveying information, which helps to accommodate students with visual disabilities like colour blindness. Ex) Dark font on light background, light font on dark background, opposing colours on colour wheel, etc.

Step 1: Select your text and choose a high contrast colour in relation to the background from the standard colour options in the Home Menu.


4. Inserting Hyperlink Text

Ensures that students who are blind and/or have low vision can read and understand what the link is meant to be used for.

Step 1: Prepare a short description of the link in a few words.



Step 2: Highlight the link's description, right click, and select "Link".



Step 3: Add in the link to the address box and select "ok".

5. Heading structure creates hierarchy

Students who use screen readers and other assistive technology navigate Word documents by heading structure. This enables students to navigate between headings and subheadings.

Step 1: Highlight the text you want to apply a heading to.

Step 2: Under Home navigate to Styles, select the type of style you would like to apply to the text. E.g. Heading 1.

Step 3: A Heading 1 is usually a page title or a main content heading. It is the most important heading, and there is generally just one. A Heading 2 is usually a major section heading. A Heading 3 is usually a sub-section of the Heading 2. A Heading 4 is usually a sub-section of the Heading 3, and so on, ending with Heading 6.

Note: One should not skip heading levels, such as using Heading 4 directly below a Heading 2.
